Flexible PCB Manufacturing: Complete Guide to Flex & Rigid-Flex Boards

Published June 10, 2026 · By Mayio Engineering Team · 8 min read

Flexible PCBs are everywhere — in your phone, your smartwatch, your earbuds, your car's dashboard. If your product needs to bend, fold, or fit into a tight space, a flex PCB is probably what you need. This guide covers everything: materials, types, pricing, design rules, and how to get them manufactured.

What Is a Flexible PCB?

A flexible PCB (flex PCB) is a circuit board built on a flexible substrate instead of rigid FR4. It can bend, fold, and conform to 3D shapes. The most common flexible substrate is polyimide (Kapton) — the same material used in spacecraft insulation.

✅ Advantages of Flex PCB

  • Bends and folds to fit any shape
  • Lighter than rigid boards
  • Higher reliability (fewer connectors = fewer failure points)
  • Thinner profile
  • Better vibration resistance

❌ Disadvantages of Flex PCB

  • 2-5x more expensive than rigid
  • Harder to design (bend radius rules)
  • More fragile during handling
  • Fewer manufacturers can make them well
  • Assembly requires more care

Flex PCB Design Rules

1. Respect the Bend Radius

The minimum bend radius depends on the number of copper layers and the total thickness:

  • Single-layer: Minimum bend radius = 6× board thickness
  • Double-layer: Minimum bend radius = 10× board thickness
  • Multi-layer: Minimum bend radius = 15× board thickness

Example: A 0.1mm thick single-layer flex board can be bent to a 0.6mm radius. Tighter than that and the copper cracks.

2. Route Traces Perpendicular to Bend Lines

Traces that run parallel to the bend direction are under maximum stress. Route them perpendicular instead. Use curved corners, not 90° angles.

3. Use Hatched Copper Pours

Solid copper pours crack when bent. Use hatched (grid) patterns instead. This allows the copper to flex without fracturing.

4. Add Stiffeners Where Needed

Connector areas and component mounting points need to be rigid. Specify stiffeners (polyimide or FR4) at these locations.

5. Account for ZIF Connectors

Most flex boards connect to rigid boards via ZIF (Zero Insertion Force) connectors. Design your flex tail to match the connector's pitch and thickness requirements.

How to Order Flex PCBs

The process is the same as ordering rigid boards — export your Gerber files and send them to your manufacturer. The key difference is that flex PCBs require more detailed specifications:

  • Material type (polyimide, PET, etc.)
  • Copper weight (typically 0.5oz or 1oz)
  • Coverlay type and thickness
  • Stiffener locations and material
  • Bend requirements (dynamic flex vs static bend)
Pro tip: Send a mechanical drawing along with your Gerber files that shows where the board needs to bend, how far, and how often. This helps the manufacturer optimize the build-up for your specific application.
